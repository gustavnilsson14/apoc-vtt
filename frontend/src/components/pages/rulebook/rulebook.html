Into the Apocalypse
A combat system for, or hack of Into the Odd

Combat happens when people disagree violently, and is resolved in the following way, usually involving bruises, blood, and death.

The following are core concepts of the combat system.

  • Combat takes place on a 1d battle mat called the Battleground. You either push forward, or fall back.
  • Combat has two measures of time. Rounds, which consists of an arbitrary amount of Turns
  • Combatants can only specify their actions at the beginning of each Round, while during this time, their actions each Turn is dictated by their planning
  • Combat ends when at the end of a Round either one side is defeated, or has fled the battleground
  • Exploiting an enemys weaknesses is important to fighting effectively
  • Likewise, using terrain, features, and hazards of the battlegrounds is equally important
  • Combatants are likely to be permanently damaged, die, or be kidnapped in the aftermath of combat, players, and their opponents alike

The Battleground

The Battleground consists of 13 tiles, where two sides can duke it out.

When the battle starts the GM first decides two tile ranges for the position of the two opposing parties,depending on the scene. Players may place their characters on any tile within the tile range they were assigned, usually spanning 3~ tiles.

Movement

Combatants may move 1 tile at the start of every round if taking the dash tactical action, then 1 tile at the start of each turn.

These rules apply to movement

  • Conscious combatants of opposing sides may not occupy the same tile.
  • Combatants of the same side may occupy the same tile, and move freely through tiles occupied by their allies.
  • A combatant cannot move past an opponent.
  • Opposing combatants moving into the same tile roll 1d12, adding their strength to the result. The lower result will be unable to move

Depending on the combatants perks and/or equipment, they may be able to move further, or ignore some rules regarding invalid movement. These rules are also designed for mainly grounded combat. Aerial combat, or underwater combat would have different rules for movement.

Features

Battlegrounds will have features, such as vantage points, explosives, cover, doorways, and whatever the GM dreams up. The location of these features should be marked on the battleground, and their stats should be noted down in advance. How to interact with these features can vary, but mostly requires a tactical action.

Common features

Fire

Deals 1d10 light damage once per turn. Dies after 2 rounds, may spread

Conductivity

Volt damage on these areas arcs, and deals 2d6 extra volt damage to all on, or in the area.

Cold

Excessively cold areas will cause combatants suffering from hypothermia to, on a failed STR, forfeit their turn.

Darkness

Fighting in darkness, or while blind, will disallow the assist action. Also, on a failed WIL, the target for any attack will be random.

Example Battleground

To use the 1d battlemat feature the only thing your group needs is;

  • 13 (or more) tiles in a line, represented on a paper
  • Miniatures (or tokens) to represent the players and the enemies
  • Tokens (or beautifully crafted props) to, represent battleground features

The players would start on the left side, and the enemies on the right, as explained above.

Battleground features and hazard tokens should be positioned on the bottom border of the battlemat, and their effects explained to the players, if applicable.

Fighting the Enemy

GM decides a range of 3~ tiles available for player characters, place minis on mat.

Round lifecycle

  1. Handle battlemat features
  2. Roll for initiative, and set combat intensity
  3. Take tactical action
  4. Planning movement and action

Turn lifecycle

  1. Handle battlemat featuress
  2. Move all combatants simultaneously.
  3. Assists and defends
  4. Ranged attack preparation
  5. Melee and Ranged attacks

Setup

As combat starts, all combatants of each respective side are placed on opposing sides, at tile #3. The GM may then, at their discretion move combatants forward, or backwards up to 2 tiles, depending on their perception of how the battle started.

First Strike

If a combatant was caught by surprise, with their weapons sheethed and guard down, the initiators may get a first strike. This allows any combatant who was combat ready as the combat starts to attack a non-combat ready combatant within range once.

Ambush

If either side ambushed the other side, seemingly springing up from nowhere, trapping or surrounding their opponents, the combat starts as an ambush.

When placing combatants on the battleground when an ambush happens, place the ambushed side on tile #6, and the ambushers on a tile #1 of their choice.

During the first round of an ambush, the ambushers choose how many turns it would last.

Rounds and Turns

A round of combat is an amount of turns decided by both the fighting parties. A turn of combat is enough time for every combatant involved to move, and perform an action.

Commitment

As a round of combat starts, the GM rolls 1d10 for initiative. If the result is below or equal to the initiative threshold, the players has it, if above the enemies have it

Turn amount starts at 3. After rolling for initiative, the side which gets it chooses to increase, or decrease the turn amount by 1.

After determining turn amount, all combatants take tactical action, then plan for the round

Tactical action (TA)

Tactical actions are special actions which can be performed by all combatants, and allows combatants to adapt to an enemies strengths, or exploit their tactics.

All combatants may use the TA's listed below, but will also have special actions available to them.

After determining the turn amount for the round, each combatant may take a tactical action

Tactical actions resolve immediately, and simultaneously.

Basic tactical actions

Aim

When attacking, choose a die and increase its result by 1 before rolling. Also allows the combatant to target opponents at range beyond the closest front.

Brace

When attacked, choose a die and reduce its result by 1 after rolling.

Cover

Only usable if on the same tile as something which could provide cover from ranged attacks. When used, all ranged attacks against the combatant have their dice reduced by 1/2/3, depending on the quality of the cover according to the GM.

Equipment

The equipment action allows a combatant to freely move items between their belt, hands and the ground, as well as either use a single consumable item from the belt or hands, throw an explosive as a ranged weapon, or move 1 item from the backpack into the hands or belt.

Catch Breath

Using the catch breath action increases your endurance by your level die.

Initiative

The initiative action increases initiative threshold by 1 when used by the player side, and reduces it by 1 when used by the enemy side

Escape

To escape the combat, roll 2d8. If the result of is less than the distance to the closest enemy, plus the amount of rounds the combat has lasted times two, the combatant leaves the combat.

Boost

The WIL check of your next spell is made at advantage, and your level damage die is added to the damage.

Cast Spell

The cast spell action allows a combatant to cast one of their spells at the end of the round, unless interrupted.

Kidnap

Kidnap an unconscious enemy combatant! O_O

Planning

At the start of each round, players plan what to do with the turns during the round.

This mainly consists of choosing where to attempt to move, which action to take and the target of said action.

The planning will decide what each combatant does each round, and cannot be changed during the round

Movement

Move to tile

A character may move towards a tile. They will move towards that tile every other turn.

Stand ground

A character may stand their ground, and forfeit their movement.

Charge

A charging character will keep attempting to move forward each turn.

Retreat

A retreating character will keep attempting to move backwards each turn.

Actions

Melee attack

Melee attacks can only be made onto adjacent tiles. They are performed each turn.

Ranged attack

Ranged attacks can be made onto any tile of the battleground unless otherwise specified by the weapon. Ranged attacks must be prepared, so they are completed every other turn. Ranged attacks cannot be made if adjacent to an enemy.

Defend

The combatant may remove one dice from any attack that targets them. Mitigates one weakness. Any saves to prevent spell interruption have advantage.

Assist

Targets a combatant. The assisted combatant rolls damage with advantage

Escape

During combat, combatants might find their morale wavering, and want to escape the fight. To accomplish this, the combatants of the escaping side must all succeed in escaping

After escaping the combatant will get away from their enemies, and automatically take a 10 minute break. After the break, the combatant will have their endurance fully restored, but might be separated from their allies, depending on how they escaped.

Spells

Spells are manipulation of the foreign energies landed on the planet, also known as magic. These all have powerful effects, and are inherently damaging to the target and volatile such as they might damage the caster. Each spell has an amount of charges, and may be used until those are depleted.

In combat casting spells is done as a tactical action. The caster decides on the amount of charges to spend, and the spell deals damage, and increases its volatility for each charge spent.

Each spell charge spent up to the skill level of the spell increases its damage by 2d8. Each charge which exceeds the skill level increases damage by 1d8.

When casting a spell, any amount of spell charges may be used. The caster must make a WIL save. Each spell charge spent reduces WIL by 1 for this check.

If the WIL save fails, the spell also deals half damage to the player, and they suffer the drained injury.

To replenish the charges of a spell, the caster must meditate at a shine dedicated to the same magic as the spell. When meditating, the spells charges is set to 1d6 + the casters skill level.

In combat, any spell can only be cast once. A spell interrupted also count towards this.

The spell is cast at the end of the round, unless interrupted by any of the following;

The caster moves in any way during the round, including forced movement like knockback The caster is hit by an attack where the attacker used the aim tactical action The caster suffers damage from a damage type they are weak against The caster is wounded

When interrupted, the caster makes a STR save. If the save fails, they lose the charges spent on the spell, and do not cast the spell at the end of the round.

Dealing damage

Attacks are basic offensive actions taken to hurt an enemy combatants. Attacks in combat hit by default.

All attacks deal two or more dice of damage. The amount of faces on the standard three dice are based on the combatants weapon, their familiarity with that specific weapon, and their experience level.

Damage Rating (DR)

Most items are weapons, and have a damage rating ranging from 0 and upwards, explained further in the weapons section.

The damage rating is the total of hollow quality slots, and effect symbols on the weapon card.

Skill Rating (SR)

A characters skill with the individual weapon affects their efficiency greatly, and can turn any weapon lethal.

Skill is tracked on the weapon card.

Explosives

When throwing an explosive with the Equipment tactical action, the explosive item deals its damage dice twice, to all creatures on the same tile. Each use of an explosive reduces its durability by 1.

Advantage

When rolling damage with advantage, any number of dice may be rerolled once, and the new result is the one used.

Guaranteed damage

Some TA's, and some weapons add guaranteed damage. This damage is added after rolling for damage, and it ignores armor.

Endurance

Endurance is a measure of how combat ready a character is. The more damage comes their way, the more endurance is spent to dodge or deflect that damage, until they are finally hit and suffer wounds.

As endurance reaches 0, it can be reduced no further, and instead the character suffers wounds, with one exception.

If any damage would result in a negative endurance less than or equal to a combatants negative max endurance, they die instantly.

Wounds

Each time a combatant is damaged when at 0 endurance they suffer a wound. The amount of wounds sustained have these effects.

Wounded

Injury

Injuries are long term damage to a characters body. A character must make a strength save, or suffer an injury when;

  • Becoming wounded, or falling unconscious in combat
  • Suffering damage out of combat
  • Being the target of a spell out of combat, in this case the check is at advantage if cast by an ally

When suffering an injury, roll 1d6 for a bodypart on your character sheet, and add a injury card to that bodypart

An injury remains until all of its slots are filled in. If the injury has been proper treated, a slot is filled after each safe rest.

Each injury reduces a characters max endurance by 8.

Armor value

Characters and creatures are either unarmored, wear armor, or have natural armor. The type, and how much armor they have determine their armor value

When attacked, each die rolled for the attack with a result less than or equal to the targets armor value deals no damage.

Weaknesses

All weapons deals damage of one or more damage types. All combatants have one or more weakness to these damage types. Figuring out an opponents weakness can be key to winning a battle

When dealing damage to an opponent with a damage type they are weak against this is considered critical damage. Dealing critical damage causes the attack to add another of the highest damage die to the attack.

Fronts

When opposing melee combatants meet on the battleground a front is created. How melee attackers are allowed to attack, and how the front moves, has specific rules.

Rules of attacking

Before any opposing combatant in a front can be the target of two attacks during the same turn, every enemy combatant must be the target of an attack. Only if your side of the front outnumber the other side, you can target the same target as one of your allies.

Inventory

All things a character carries with them which are of note are represented by a item card. Item cards represent any weapons, protection, or tools the character carries and have at their disposal. The character sheet, and vehicle sheets has dedicated slots to contain item cards, which also denote how the item is carried.

Items carried in the characters hands or belt can be used in combat, while items carried on the characters body are items worn or strapped to them, and items in the 3 inventory slots are items stored in a backpack.

Item types

There are six different types of items, each with their own icons and usage.

  • Melee weapon
  • Ranged weapon
  • Shield
  • Headgear
  • Armor
  • Tool

Stats

All items have stats, but depending on the type of the item the stats may mean different things.

Quality

The round slots are called quality. The quality of an item counts as both a durability, and an effect icon. Filling in the quality slot as the result of damaging the item will also result in the quality being lost for effect purposes.

Durability

The durability of an item is a measure of how much damage the item can withstand. When all durability, and quality slots are filled in as a result of damage, an item is destroyed.

Effect

The effect of an item counts towards either the damage rating, or armor value of the item. Quality also adds to this

Item damage

When an item is either used for somthing it is not intended for, suffers rough physical treatment, is partially or completely consumed, or due to combat, it takes damage.

When an item takes damage, cross out the first durability or quality slot (starting from the top left) which is not yet filled in.

Some items, at the GM's discretion are not destroyed when all durability or quality slots are filled in, and may be recharged or refilled, although this is an exception

Combat damage

After combat, roll 1d6 for each item used that combat. If the die result is less than or equal to the amount of rounds, during which that it was used that combat, it loses one durability

Repair

Repairing a weapon may restore sturdy quality slots. Repairing a quality slot will take some time, and resources, usually worth around ~75% of what the quality slot would cost if new.

Weapons

There is a variety of weapons out there, from knives and clubs, to guns and grenades. The following is true for a weapon;

  • Weapons add a damage die to each attack. Which die depends on their damage rating
  • All weapons have a card, and needs to be held by a character to be used
  • Weapons have several damage types. If a weapon hits an enemy and has a damage type that combatant is weak against, it deals critical damage
  • There are two types of weapons, melee and ranged
  • Quality and effect increases the weapons damage rating by 1
  • A weapons skill is used to track the players skill swith the individual weapon.

Damage Rating

The damage rating of a weapon is calculated by adding its damage (plus signs) to its unfilled quality (circle slots).

The damage rating determines the weapons damage die. Damage ratings over 5 add +1 to the die roll.

Protection

Different types of armor comes in shields, helmets, bodywear, or complete outfits. The following statements are true about armor;

  • There are four types of protection, armor, headgear, and shield
  • Each quality and effect increases armor value by 1
  • Quality is also used as durability for protection
  • The damage types of a protection item determines weaknesses the it mitigates
  • Armor occupying two card slots will occupy a characters body body item slots

Armor value

The total armor value of all equipped protection is added together when resolving the effect of an attack.

Each damage die equal to or less than armor value has no effect

Weakness mitigation

All damage types listed with a protection will mitigate the wearers weakness to that damage type. Additionally, the wearer can never become weak to that damage type as long as they wear the armor.

Tool

Tools are a common name for anything not a weapon, or protection. It is anything between batteries, water bottles, food, and egg timers.

Tools only has durability. Durability is reduced when tools is consumed, or used for something it is not made for

Character creation

First, to create a character requires you to make 3 rolls, one for each attribute, note them down on the character sheet.

Second, calculate your maximum endurance, and note that down on your character sheet. Formula is found below.

Third, use the big roll button in the backgrounds section to determine your background

Fourth, roll for your characters weaknesses, explained below

Fifth, pick one optional tactical action from the skills, or gambits table for your character. Then roll one random tactical action from the opposite table.

Sixth, buy your characters starting gear, also explained below.

Attributes

These constitute your characters benefits and burdens, and is the base for any skill related die rolls you might undertake. The attributes are;

Strength, STR

Physical might, pushing and pulling, lifting and bending, enduring and powering through.

Dexterity, DEX

Speed, agility, coordination, flexibility, and grace.

Willpower, WIL

Force of will, spiritual power, and ability to focus.

Rolling and assigning

Roll 3d6 for each attribute and choose two of the dice to add together for the result.

Endurance

20+(character level * 10)+STR+DEX+WIL

Character weaknesses

Your character will have up to 6 weaknesses to damage types. Roll 6d12 on the damage types table above, and note down every distinct result. A character cannot have the same weakness twice, so rolling more of the same number on several dice will result in less damage types weaknesses

The amount of weaknesses your character has translates to their appearance. Damage weakness = HAWT!

Starting gear

Your character starts with the following items

  • 1 Water, containing 3 liters. A character drinks 1 liter per day, and water is also currency.
  • 1 Rations
  • The characters amount of weaknesses times 7 as purchase points

Purchase points

These only exist in character creation, and allows the character to buy gear. The prices are;

  • Weapons and armor cost 1 point per durability, 3 per effect, and 4 per quality.
  • Tools cost 2 points if generally useful, 1 if non-impactful.
  • Spells cost 6 points.
  • Random mutations cost 4 points.
  • Purchase points can be spent as experience for the purpose of increaing the skill level of weapons

Relationships

Your character does not exist in a vaccum, and will have bonds with friends and family, as well as grudges with adversaries and wrongdoers.

A characters relations should be regularly used by the GM to create engaging play for your character, but it only works if you as a player embrace and lean into those relationships. Bonds should be treasures by your character, and grudges should be persued.

Your characters interactions with their relationships are not always logical, but driven by emotion. Your character will act in a more reckless manner when protecting their bonds, or persuing their grudges.

The combatant may target an enemy allowed by their current weaponry. On a successful STR, the target must choose an equipped armor or weapon which loses 1 durability

Feign death

On a successful DEX the combatant appears dead, and will appear so until moving, taking tactical, or regular action

Intimidate

The combatant intimidates any target. This round, each turn on a failed WIL, the target cannot move towards the combatant. If they fail all the WIL checks during the round, their next TA will automatically be to try and escape.

Disarm

The combatant targets a melee opponent. On a failed STR, the combatant chooses an item held by the target that they drop.

Suppression

If the combatant is using the ranged attack action. This round, each turn, the ranged attack target loses their movement on a failed DEX

Threaten

The combatant chooses any target to threaten. The targets armor is reduced by 1 on a failed WIL

Protect

On successful DEX, the combatant stands in the way of an ally on the same tile, taking all damage directed at the ally.

Advancement

While travelling the wastes, characters gain experience, as a measure of lessons learned and knowledge gained in their adventures. Experience can be spent on two things;

  • Between game sessions, spending experience equal to their characters level * 6 to increase their level
  • Between game sessions, the skill in a weapon may be increased if spending the experience equal to the current skill level + 1

Leveling up

As a character levels up, they become stronger and learn new tricks. They will carry themselves with more poise, and be treated by their surroundings as such.

When leveling up, your character will gain;

  • An attribute point for you to increase one of your attributes by 1
  • An additional 10 endurance, as per the calculation for max endurance
  • A tactical action of your choice

Aquiring experience

Experience is gotten in the following ways;

  • 1 Per game session, regardless of the session.
  • Making in-game player character plans prior to a session, communicating player agenda them to the GM.
  • Acting table secretary, taking notes on NPC's, locations, etc.
  • Keeping track of rounds passed, turn order, hazards, and damage dealt to enemies during combat.
  • Taking an active part in irl game scheduling.
  • Bringing snacks to the table.
